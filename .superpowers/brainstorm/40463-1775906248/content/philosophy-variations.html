Variation 1 — Refined current structure

Our philosophy

Your attention is the only resource that doesn't renew.

No streaks turning curiosity into obligation.

No feed engineered to outlast your willpower.

Five ideas worth knowing. Finished.

The way good things have always worked.

Knowledge that compounds. Slowly. Reliably.

The way the best things always have.

↑ click to replay

Closest to today. Fixes "ten things" → "five ideas." Replaces the opening line with something sharper — attention as a non-renewable resource. Everything else stays. Safe, coherent, on-brand.

Variation 2 — Bold and sparse (fewer, heavier lines)

Our philosophy

The best ideas deserve more than a scroll.

Morning gives you five — then gets out of the way.

No algorithm. No streak. No infinite.

Just something worth knowing, every day.

↑ click to replay

Most confident. Four lines, no filler. Each one earns its place. "Gets out of the way" is a strong product promise — rare in an industry that competes on engagement time. Punchy but risks feeling too short.

Variation 3 — Provocation that resolves into Morning

Our philosophy

You are not distracted.
You are being distracted.

There's a difference. One is a character flaw. The other is a design choice — made by people whose business depends on your attention never ending.

Morning is built on the opposite premise.

Finite by design No streaks No feed Done when you're done Knowledge, not content

↑ click to replay

Most provocative. Opens with an accusation — but at the industry, not the reader. The distinction "distracted vs being distracted" is sharp. The pills at the end are a different format; could feel off-brand. Best if Morning wants to take a stronger stance.

Variation 4 — Numbered declarations

Our philosophy

01 Attention is finite. Treat it that way.
02 No streaks. No guilt. No algorithm.
03 Five ideas. Done. Every morning.
04 Knowledge compounds. Slowly. Reliably.
05 The way the best things always have.

↑ click to replay

Most structured. Numbered lines feel like principles, not poetry. The numbers add visual rhythm without cluttering. Strong/muted alternation still creates hierarchy. Familiar editorial format — think annual reports, manifestos, design principles pages.
