Contrast — Four directions

All four keep the same truth: you're wasting mornings, Morning doesn't. They differ in structure, copy register, and how hard they lean into the contrast.

Direction A — Two-column, current layout, sharpened copy

The rest of the internet

You open your phone.

An hour disappears.

You couldn't tell anyone why.

Morning

You read five ideas.

You close the app.

You carry one with you all day.

Same layout as today — no structural change. New copy is more concrete and cinematic. "An hour disappears" is something everyone knows. "Couldn't tell anyone why" is the guilt that lands.

Direction B — Stacked pairs: the old way → the Morning way

Most mornings  →  Morning

You scroll to feel informed

→

You read to actually know

You forget by lunchtime

→

You're still thinking about it tonight

The algorithm decides what you see

→

Five ideas, curated with care

Most structurally different. Row-by-row pairing makes each contrast feel deliberate. The strikethrough on the left reads as "crossed out, replaced." Works well on mobile. Feels more editorial.

Direction C — One statement. No columns.

The difference

Most apps are designed to keep you scrolling.
Morning is designed to let you stop.

No feed. No streak. No infinite scroll. Just five ideas worth knowing — then you're done for the day.

Most minimal. Drops the two-column structure entirely. One sentence does all the work. Feels like a manifesto line rather than a product comparison. Risk: less punchy than the side-by-side contrast.

Direction D — Two panels with tags: what you lose vs what you gain

The old morning

You open your phone before you've said a word. You reach for stimulation before you've had a thought. Forty minutes pass. You've learned the shape of other people's opinions and nothing else.

The Morning morning

Five ideas. Chosen carefully. From Health, Money, Tech, Psychology, and the World. Read at your own pace. Done when you're done.

Then the phone goes down. The idea stays with you.

Most descriptive. Paints a fuller picture — almost micro-essay style. Fits Morning's editorial voice. Risks being too long if the user doesn't read. Best for people who are already curious about the product.
